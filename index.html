1. Introduction

1.1   Background on Industrial Vibration Monitoring

Industrial vibration monitoring plays a crucial role in many industries, from marine, oil and gas, manufacturing industries, to space and aircraft industries (Mohd Ghazali, Mohamad Hazwan, Rahiman, & Wan, 2021).

Reliability and efficiency of these machines are of utmost importance for continuous, uninterrupted running which minimises future maintenance costs. One of the most widely used modern techniques for condition monitoring is through vibration analysis, allowing for early detection of mechanical faults in rotating machinery such as, imbalance, misalignment and bearing failures.

The current vibration monitoring systems in the market are often expensive and requires specialised equipment and expertise. However, this limits accessibility especially for small medium enterprises. Through technological evolution, embedded systems and low-cost computing platforms such as Raspberry Pi 5, create an opportunity to develop affordable yet effective monitoring systems.

 

1.2   Problem Statement

Despite adopting vibration-based condition monitoring systems, there are many gaps in the current market solutions, such as accessibility and adaptability, particularly in marine and industrial rotating machinery systems (Chu, Nguyen, Yoo, & Wang, 2024).

In marine vessels and industrial plants, rotating machinery forms the backbone of power generation for essential critical operations. One of the most critical equipment onboard or in plants, are alternators. Alternators are the “heart” of a vessel or plant (Bright Hub Engineering, 2021). For marine alternators, supplying electrical power for navigation, propulsion support systems and onboard services. For plant alternators, supplying electrical power for plant’s internal operations depending on load requirements, ensuring stable energy output for machinery and process control systems, among other functions.

In a vessel/plant, there are multiple alternators so load can be placed on a different working alternator, should any breakdown. However, this avoids the issue, and the vessel/plant would have one less alternator, leading to a potentially dangerous scenario, where the other alternators might have issues too and breakdown. This results in downtime for the vessel/plant, and for vessel crew to be stranded at sea, as without alternators propulsion systems are disrupted.

Ultimately, alternators would have to be repaired, as using the remaining alternators while leaving one permanently down, is neither professional nor legal, due to class checks and audits for vessel/plants (Daya & Lazakis, 2024). However, cost of repair for alternators are expensive, due to extensive work required (E. Koh, personal communication, March 2026). Repair process of alternator found in Appendix under A1.

While repair and transportation are costly, downtime costs vessels/plants the greatest (Daya & Lazakis, 2024; Jardine, Lin, & Banjevic, 2006). Unplanned downtimes cost Global 2000 $400 billion annually (Arda, 2026). In this period, employees’ salaries are paid, while being unable to transport cargo (for vessels) or unable to produce goods (for plants). Such downtime disrupts supply chains and logistics, delaying delivery commitments and project deadlines. This may have a cascading effect where reputation is damaged and customers lose trust. For vessel downtime, it might affect port schedule, leading to legal implications. Vessel/plant management would want the alternator(s) to be repaired quickly, paying higher than market rate to have it repaired urgently.

With the critical issues highlighted above, come the market solution of vibration-based condition monitoring for predictive maintenance. Yet, despite availability of such systems, there are a few issues:

-        High cost of commercial condition monitoring systems

-        Limited adaptability to different machines and environments

-        Lack of real-time, user-friendly visualisation tools

-        Requires a trained analyst to determine issues from spectrum

Furthermore, many existing solutions rely purely on threshold-based methods, which have limitations in terms of robustness and accurate prediction of errors.

Threshold-based methods are effective for detection of severe faults under stable operating conditions (Zhang & Ganesan, 2006). However, in real-world environments such as in vessel/plants, operating conditions may be dynamic due to variable loads and environmental influences. In such cases, the threshold-based approach may lead to false alarms or even missed early-stage faults. The Takagi-Sugeno-Kang (TSK) fuzzy inference system enhances robustness of the device in fault detection by modelling gradual transition between steady-state and transient states, allowing for more reliable detection under varying conditions (Haykin, 2009).

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

1.3   Value Proposition of AltGuard

AltGuard is proposed as a low-cost, real-time vibration monitoring system that addresses challenges through a hybrid approach.

The system integrates:

-        Low-cost sensing setup using ADXL356 and ADS1115

-        Real-time signal processing and feature extraction

-        Hybrid fault detection algorithm combining threshold-based logic and TSK fuzzy inference

-        Web-based dashboard for live monitoring and visualization

The novelty of Altguard lies in its ability to:

-        Target real-world rotating machinery applications (alternators in offshore marine/ onshore plants)

-        Adapt to baseline machine behaviour using adaptive thresholds

-        Differentiate between transient and steady-state conditions, improving reliability of fault detection

-        Provide interpretable, real-time insights to users

The signal processing pipeline is designed to extract meaningful features from raw vibration data.

The key steps include:

1.     Signal acquisition and preprocessing

2.     Windowing and segmentation

3.     Time-domain feature extraction

4.     Feature-domain analysis using Fast Fourier Transformation (FFT)

The FFT is particularly important in identifying dominant frequencies and harmonic components, indicating specific mechanical faults.

 

 

 

 

 

3.3   Feature Extraction Techniques

Several features are extracted to characterise the vibration signal:

A)   Root Mean Square (RMS): Indicates overall vibration energy. RMS is a baseline indicator that is widely used in vibration monitoring systems. It provides a simple but effective health metric of the overall system (Viking Analytics, 2024).

B)    Kurtosis: Measures impulsiveness of signal, which can indicate sharp peaks in vibration, linking to bearing or gear faults (MathWorks, n.d.).

C)    Dominant Frequency: Identifies primary vibration frequency for frequency spectrum analysis via FFT. This is standard in condition monitoring, with dominant frequency trend used for diagnosis (Viking Analytics, 2024).

D)   Harmonic Ratio: Detects presence of harmonics for diagnosing misalignments and mechanical looseness. This is a key feature is predictive maintenance and signal processing systems (Towards Data Science, 2023).

These features are used as inputs for both threshold-based and fuzzy classification models.

 

3.4   Fault Classification Models (Threshold vs Fuzzy Logic)

Fault classification in vibration-based condition monitoring can be achieved using one of the following methods: Deterministic threshold-based method or Intelligent inference model.

 

Threshold-based classification

This is a widely used approach in industrial monitoring systems as it is both simple and reliable. In this method, predefined, hardcoded limits are set for key vibration features such as RMS, kurtosis and harmonic ratio.

For example:

-        High RMS values may indicate imbalance

-        Elevated kurtosis values suggest impulsive events such as bearing faults

-        Increased harmonic ratios could indicate misalignment

The advantage in this classification method lies in its ease of interpretability, where there are set guidelines that these values should fall under (Jardine, Lin, & Banjevic, 2006). If they are above the thresholds, it is easy to pinpoint faults that lie within the system. However, it is limited by its lack of generalisability across different machines or operating systems. Furthermore, values that lies on the borderline of these thresholds, are not flagged by the system. This may cause potential sudden breakdown of monitored equipment due to rapid degradation. To improve on its weaknesses, we can use adaptive thresholds that are dynamically learned from baseline data through machine learning.

 

Fuzzy Logic Classification (TSK Model)

To overcome the limitations of rigid thresholds, a TSK fuzzy inference system is implemented (Zadeh, 1996).

Fuzzy logic allows for:

-        Gradual transition between fault states

-        Handling of uncertainty in signal features

-        More flexible decision-making

In the TSK model, input features, such as RMS, kurtosis, harmonic ratio, are mapped to fuzzy membership functions (e.g. low, medium, high). These memberships are then combined through a rule base to generate a weighted output representing the machine condition.

For example:

-        High RMS with low harmonic content à Imbalance

-        High harmonic ratio à Misalignment

-        High kurtosis à Bearing fault

Unlike Mamdani fuzzy systems, the TSK approach produces a mathematical output via weighted average, making it computationally efficient for real-time embedded systems (Zadeh, 1996).

 

Hybrid Classification Approach

The AltGuard system integrates both methods into a hybrid framework:

-        During transient conditions, threshold-based classification is prioritised for rapid and reliable detection.

-        During steady-state conditions, TSK fuzzy inference is used for more nuanced and adaptive classification.

This dual-layer approach improves overall system robustness by combining strengths of both deterministic threshold and intelligent inference methods.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

3.5   Literature Review

Vibration-based condition monitoring has been extensively studied as a non-invasive method to detect faults in rotating machinery. It relies on the principle that mechanical defects produce characteristic changes in vibration signals. This can be extracted and analysed in both time and frequency domains.

 

Time-domain Feature Analysis

Specific time-domain features such as RMS and kurtosis are commonly used in fault detection (Viking Analytics, 2024). RMS represents the overall energy of the vibration signal and used in identifying imbalance. Kurtosis, measures impulsiveness of a signal and used to detect bearing faults due to its sensitivity to transient spikes.

 

Frequency-Domain Analysis

Frequency-domain analysis using FFT is a fundamental technique in vibration diagnosis. It allows the identification of dominant frequencies and harmonic components (Haykin, 2009).

For instance:

-        Imbalance: Characterised by strong peak at fundamental rotational frequency (FRF)

-        Misalignment: Strong peak at FRF and at additional higher harmonics of 2x, 3x

-        Bearing defects: Broadband frequency components

 

Intelligent Fault Classification Methods

Fuzzy logic is a machine learning technique that is particularly useful, as it can handle uncertainty in measurements. Additionally, it is a robust and flexible way of providing interpretable rules which mimics engineering logic to guide the system in correctly classifying faults.

There are two main fuzzy inference system (FIS) models: Mamdani and TSK (Mamdani & Assilian, 1975; Takagi & Sugeno, 1985). While they are both fuzzy logic frameworks, they differ in handling outputs. TSK model is used in preference over Mamdani model as:

-        Interpretability: TSK model is more mathematical and efficient, suited for analysis and adaptive models, while Mamdani is more intuitive and rule-based, which is close to human reasoning.

-        Complexity: TSK model is computationally lighter and faster, however, Mamdani is computationally heavier due to defuzzification.

 

 

3.6   Research Gap

Despite advancements in vibration monitoring systems, several gaps remain:

-        Many systems rely heavily on threshold-based methods

-        Fixed thresholds are often unable to adapt to varying machine conditions

-        Requires manual in-depth analysis by trained specialists

-        Real-time, low-cost implementations using embedded systems are still limited

This project addresses such gaps by developing a hybrid system, integrating threshold-based rules and fuzzy inference methods, and adaptive learning, on a low-cost embedded platform, for automated reliable real-time alerts.

4.2   Selection of Components

Selection of components was based on cost due to limited budget. However, performance is another key priority, as vibration monitoring systems are crucial for a machine’s life cycle and needs to accurately detect subtle faults early.

 

Accelerometer Selection

The ADXL356 was selected due to:

-        High sensitivity suitable for vibration monitoring

-      Ultra-low noise density of , enabling detection of subtle early faults

-        Wide dynamic range, allowing detection of both small and large vibrations

Another accelerometer that was considered is the ADXL345. Although it is a digital accelerometer providing direct data output, and a lot cheaper than the ADXL356 (by roughly 65SGD), it was not selected as:

-        Noise performance is moderate and less stable than ADXL356, with higher noise density of , making it suitable for regular consumer devices. However, for critical industrial usage, noise density should be as low as possible for most precise condition monitoring.

-      ADXL345 has a lower range from to which are tuned for human-scale motion. However, ADXL356 has a range of or , designed for industrial vibration and machinery condition monitoring, suiting AltGuard’s objectives.

-        Compared to digital accelerometers, analog output provides greater flexibility in signal analysis.

Furthermore, ADXL356 was chosen instead of other analog accelerometers such as MMA7361, as ADXL356 is a multi-axial accelerometer. Placing it at a global localised point or area would be an advantage since there is no need to change its location. Changing locations may give rise to variability in surfaces, thus affecting its accuracy.

ADC Selection

The ADS1115 was chosen for its:

-        High resolution (16-bit), allowing precise measurement and hence, the detection of, small signal variations

-        Compatibility with I2C communication, simplifying integration with the Raspberry Pi

-        Cost-effectiveness compared to industrial-grade ADC systems

 

Processing Unit Selection

Raspberry Pi 5 was selected in comparison to the Pi 4 due to:

-        CPU/GPU Performance: Pi 5 uses a quad-core ARM Cortex-A76 at 2.4 GHz while the Pi 4’s Cortex-A72 at 1.6 GHz. This means that the Pi5 is roughly two to three times faster in benchmarks. High-frequency data can be acquired by the Pi 5 faster and it can deal with higher sampling rates without bottlenecks.

-        Memory Bandwidth: Pi 5 has LLPDDR4X RAM with higher throughput, improving multitasking and data-heavy workloads.

-        Storage Speed: Pi 5 supports PCIe for NVMe SSDs, offering much faster read and write speeds as compared to Pi 4’s microSD-only setup

-        Thermal management: Pi 5 comes with active cooling support and a power button, which helps to support continuous operation, however, Pi 4 runs hot under load. Thus, Pi 5 is better suited for 24/7 monitoring.

Despite Pi 5 costing around two times the price and consuming more power than Pi 4, the benefits that Pi 5 provides, greatly outweighs that of Pi 4, when considering the context of high-frequency vibration-based condition monitoring, that relies heavily on processing real-time data.

 

Connector Considerations

Molex connectors are considered over Dupont connectors as:

-        Molex connectors are more reliable and durable.

-        Molex connectors produce less noise, due to sturdier connection

These connectors may be a small component of the system, however, it affects accuracy and precision. Choosing a cheaper but noisier connector such as Dupont connectors, may lead to future errors when testing actual machinery.

 

 

 

Device Casing Considerations

Figure 16: 3D Model of AltGuard System Hardware Layout

To suit marine offshore vessels/ onshore industrial plants, the device must be encased appropriately to match the environments.

Through thorough research, AltGuard’s case should be IP67 (IEC 60529). IP67 is dust-tight, preventing not only dust, but salt crystals in marine environment or fine particles in plants, from entering the device with the sensor and ADC. Contamination may lead to potential noisy artefacts that degrade the system’s accuracy.

Potential scenarios where water enters the plant/vessel, resulting in device immersion, IP67 rating prevents the interior, sensor and ADC, from coming in contact with water, thus preventing damage.

As saltwater is highly corrosive, IP67 sealed housing largely prevents ingress and prevents long-term degradation due to its corrosion-resistant material.

Furthermore, the case can be ruggedised to withstand harsh working conditions. For example, polycarbonate casing is budget-friendly yet provides effective protection.

8. Conclusion

AltGuard addresses the need for a low-cost, real-time vibration-based condition monitoring system for rotating machinery, specifically targeting marine and industrial alternators, where urgent repair and downtime can be costly and disruptive. As existing market solutions are often expensive and rely heavily on threshold-based methods, AltGuard introduces a hybrid approach combining adaptive thresholds with TSK fuzzy logic, enabling robustness for more accurate fault detection.

At the current stage, the project has successfully established a functional software architecture, including signal preprocessing, feature extract and preliminary fault classification. Simulations of different faults were also tested with the system and can be displayed on a web-based dashboard for visualisation.

Moving forward, the project focuses on integrated system testing, after all hardware integration. A1)   More information on Alternator Repair

The alternator is first lifted out from a tight engine room in a vessel or from a corner of the plant. Both locations require a lot of manpower, and it is dangerous, as one wrong lift could result in loss of lives or physical damage to the alternator. Afterwards, transport arrangements are made to a workshop. Then, the damaged alternator is repaired within a timeframe of usually 2 weeks. Following this, the repaired alternator is sent back to the vessel/plant for reinstatement and commissioning. The whole process is costly and time consuming (RTS Marine, n.d.).

A2)   Further information on On-Machine Processing (Edge computing)

Signal preprocessing

This is implemented in processing.py within extract_features() function:

    signal = signal - np.mean(signal)

    window = np.hanning(N)

    windowed = signal * window

This removes DC offset from the signal and applies a Hanning window to reduce spectral leakage. The Hanning window was chosen instead of other windows as it is able to strike a balance between leakage reduction and frequency resolution, thus making it a strong choice for vibration-based condition monitoring (Harris, 1978).

 

Feature extraction

Feature extraction is also implemented within preprocessing.py:

    rms = np.sqrt(np.mean(signal**2))

    kurt = kurtosis(signal, fisher=False)

    fft_vals = np.fft.fft(windowed, n=N_fft)

    freqs = np.fft.fftfreq(N_fft, 1/fs)[:N_fft//2]

From these, the system is able to compute RMS, kurtosis, dominant frequency and harmonic ratio.

RMS measures the overall energy level of the vibration signal. The higher the RMS, the more intense the vibration, which is useful for detecting changes in load, which will be covered next, or in detecting severity of vibration.

Kurtosis measures how “peaked” or “flat” the distribution is. In vibration analysis, high kurtosis indicated high impulsiveness of the occurring event, for example, bearing fault.

The bottom two codes are for FFT. “fft_vals” gives the amplitude spectrum, while “freqs” gives the frequency bins. This converts the time-domain signal into frequency domain, revealing dominant vibration frequencies, which can be used to classify faults.

 

Transient and Steady-state detection

Transient and steady-state detection are implemented in processing.py under detect_transient():

cv = np.std(rms_history) / mean_rms

return "Transient" if cv > cv_threshold else "Steady"

CV stands for coefficient of variation. This normalises variability relative to the average. This means that if the vibration level fluctuates greatly, this shows the machinery is in an unstable, changing transient state. Whereas, in a steady-state, it is in stable operation, where the CV should be below the threshold.

This determines whether the machine is operating in steady-state or transient conditions.

Spectral analysis was also considered to determine state of machinery, where FFT is used to detect frequency-domain changes, but it is heavier computationally. The CV method is lightweight and easy to implement, while effective for real-time monitoring (Jalilibal, Amiri, Castagliola, & Khoo, 2021).

 

Fault classification (Threshold-based and fuzzy logic)

Threshold-based classification

This is implemented in processing.py as well and is handled by threshold_classification():

if thresholds and thresholds.trained:

    rms_th = thresholds.get_threshold('rms', k=3.0)

    kurt_th = thresholds.get_threshold('kurtosis', k=3.0)

    harm_th = thresholds.get_threshold('harmonic_ratio', k=3.0)

else:

    rms_th = config.FALLBACK_RMS_HIGH

    kurt_th = config.FALLBACK_KURT_HIGH

    harm_th = config.FALLBACK_HARMONIC_HIGH

“thresholds” is an instance of AdaptiveThresholds from utilis.py. It learns baseline statistics for mean and standard deviation from a training period when the machine is healthy. When “thresholds” exist and finished training, the dynamic thresholds can be computed and here k=3 means the alarm level is 3 standard deviations above healthy mean, which is a common statistical process control limit. However, if no adaptive thresholds are available, the code falls back on hard-coded constants from config.py, as a safety net.

Fixed thresholds might not work for all machines as such, adaptive thresholds are in place to allow the system to calibrate itself to the true vibration levels of that specific machine.

    confidences = {}

    if features['kurtosis'] > kurt_th:

        confidences['Bearing Fault'] = min((features['kurtosis'] - kurt_th) / kurt_th, 1.0)

    if features['harmonic_ratio'] > harm_th:

        confidences['Misalignment'] = min((features['harmonic_ratio'] - harm_th) / harm_th, 1.0)

    if features['rms'] > rms_th and features['harmonic_ratio'] < 0.2:

        confidences['Imbalance'] = min((features['rms'] - rms_th) / rms_th, 1.0)

For each fault type, we check if the relevant feature exceed thresholds which computes a confidence between 0 and 1.0, showing how far the value exceeds the thresholds.

For ‘imbalance’, there is an extra condition harmonic_ratio < 0.2. This is because a high RMS alone could be caused by misalignment or bearing fault. Misalignment presents itself with a strong 2x harmonic, but if the harmonic ratio is low, such as in the above condition, then imbalance is likely the cause instead.

 

TSK Fuzzy Inference System Classification

The fuzzy logic is implemented in fuzzy.py. Some of the vital codes will be displayed and explained below.

def gaussian(x, mean, sigma):

    """Gaussian membership function."""

    return np.exp(-0.5 * ((x - mean) / sigma) ** 2)

The classic Gaussian bell curve is used for AltGuard as it is smooth, continuous and also has natural decay, which has advantages in representing vague concepts such as “low” or  “high” without sharp transitions.

    # Rule 1: Low everything → Normal

    w1 = rms_low(rms) * kurt_low(kurt) * harm_low(harmonic)

    rules.append((w1, 0))

 

 

    # Rule 2: High RMS, low harmonic → Imbalance

    w2 = rms_high(rms) * harm_low(harmonic)

    rules.append((w2, 1))

    # Rule 3: High harmonic ratio → Misalignment

    w3 = harm_high(harmonic)

    rules.append((w3, 2))

 

    # Rule 4: High kurtosis → Bearing fault

    w4 = kurt_high(kurt)

    rules.append((w4, 3))

The rule strengths w1 to w4, is the product of membership values.

A zero-order TSK is used as the consequent is a constant and is simpler to tune. Additionally, it is effective for classification.

    numerator = sum(w * output for w, output in rules)

    denominator = sum(w for w, _ in rules)

    fault_index = numerator / denominator

This gives the weighted average of the rule outputs and is a cript number between 0 and 3.

    confidence = denominator / len(rules)

    states = ["Normal", "Imbalance", "Misalignment", "Bearing Fault"]

    final_state = states[int(round(fault_index))]

    return final_state, confidence

“confidence” gives us the fraction of total possible rule activation where it measures how well the input matches the rule base.

Finally, the final_state can be deduced from the fault_index, which shows one of the states above, “normal”, “imbalance”, “misalignment” or “bearing fault”.

This approach is very useful for AltGuard as it helps to handle uncertainty and unfamiliar machinery. Additionally, it is a lightweight code, with easy computations that can run easily on the Raspberry Pi. Furthermore, the confidence score allows the system to know when it is not certain, hence overriding TSK system with threshold rules during transient states.
